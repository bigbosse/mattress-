### The Science of Darkness and Sleep Quality

The human body relies on circadian rhythms to regulate sleep-wake cycles. Exposure to artificial light—whether from streetlamps, electronics, or alarm clocks—suppresses the production of melatonin, the hormone responsible for sleep. For a snorer, whose sleep is already fragmented by breathing interruptions, light pollution acts as a secondary disruptor that prevents the brain from entering deep REM cycles.

Using **Blackout Sleep Masks for Snorers** creates an environment of total darkness. This signals the brain that it is time to rest, helping to offset the micro-awakenings caused by snoring. By controlling the light environment, individuals can improve their "sleep efficiency," which is the percentage of time spent asleep while in bed.

Many snorers utilize additional devices, such as nasal strips, mouth tape, or Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP) machines.
